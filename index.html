A study of household size and income in the city of Barcelona
Using data from Barcelona, we examined the relationship between the number of people living in a household and income, revealing a U-shaped pattern. Both lower- and higher-income individuals tend to live in households with more people, while middle-income individuals typically inhabit households with fewer members. Our results support the idea that wealth and household sizes do not follow a monotonic relationship.
Keywords: Data analysis, Python
An exploration of ethnicity and baby naming trends in New York City
Names given to babies in New York City from 2011 to 2019 were studied. We focused on the differences across four ethnic groups: Asian-Pacific Islander, Black, Hispanic, and White. By introducing a measure of naming overlap based on cosine similarity, the project highlights which communities share similar naming conventions and which have distinct preferences, as well as how naming trends evolved throughout the 2010s.
Keywords: Data analysis, Python
Dashboard of European academic funding
This dashboard presents the extent to which, from 2007 to 2022, different European countries and metropolitan areas have benefitted from the grants awarded by the European Research Council, the main academic research funding body of the European Union. Data is presented in absolute terms and per capita, and we compute a Gini coefficient to reveal the extent to which grants awarded within a country are monopolized by just a few cities.
Keywords: Data visualization, Tableau
Visualization of Greater Tokyo figures
We often fail to grasp the sheer numbers of Greater Tokyo, the most populous metropolitan area in the world. This visualization presents three Voronoi diagrams that equate the surface, population and GDP of Greater Tokyo to the combined metrics of numerous sovereign states.
Keywords: Data visualization, Tableau
Exploration-exploitation choices and the hot stove effect: a simulation-based approach
We simulated decision-making agents choosing between two alternatives while balancing exploitation and exploration in order to maximize reward. By varying the temperature parameter in a softmax algorithm, the agents show differing rates of selecting the most rewarding alternative. The research highlights how the algorithm naturally gives way to the hot stove effect, where initial negative outcomes lead to avoiding further choices of an alternative, causing the underestimation of it value.
Keywords: Simulations, R
Relating country differences in budget expenditure to socio-economic indicators
Machine learning techniques were used to relate the way countries spend their budget to socio-economic indicators. Initial k-means clustering did not reveal meaningful country groupings, but Principal Component Analysis (PCA) identified different trends, such as the correlation between higher spending on health and social protection and lower spending on bureaucratic services. Our main principal component was strongly linked to the Human Development Index (HDI) and moderately correlated with the proportion of public employees in the workforce.
Keywords: Machine learning, Python
Predicting a wine's quality from its physicochemical properties
Machine learning models were used to predict wine quality based on physicochemical properties from Portuguese vinho verde wines. Each model's performance was optimized via hyperparameter tuning through grid search and cross-validation. Among various models, including linear and tree-based approaches, a random forest model demonstrated the best performance. Key predictors of higher quality ratings included higher alcohol content, lower density, and reduced volatile acidity.
Keywords: Machine learning, Python
